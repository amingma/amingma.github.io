On Debugging Well

Whether you’re someone who is just beginning their programming journey or an extremely seasoned developer, the issue of debugging code is definitely an universally frustrating experience. Personally speaking, I have way too many memories of just blankly staring at a computer screen, completely lost as to why my code isn’t running as intended. Fortunately, I also believe these experiences have given me some insight into the debugging process, so I wanted to share a few general suggestions on how we can hopefully debug less and debug happier.

Don't underestimate a warm up

I played tennis in high school, and we would spend a good deal of time warming up with dynamic exercises and stretches to get our bodies loose and ready for the practice ahead. It might seem strange to bring the same practice into the sedentary environment of programming, but I've actually found it pretty helpful to move around a little and get the heart rate up before starting lengthy assignments. I think an engaged body translates to an engaged mind, which is important because of the focus and thinking coding so often demands. Of course, remember to take breaks in between as well.

Preparing to fail is not a failure in preparation

This probably sounds confusing, but hear me out. No matter how precise or careful we think we are being, the truth is that bugs are an unavoidable aspect of programming. Therefore, we should code in a way that anticipates issues that have not appeared and is considerate towards our future self. By writing clean code that is documented by comments, we can save a lot of time and energy for when those bugs inevitably pop up.

Take accountability

Personally, I've had an embarrassingly large number of instances where I refused to believe I made a mistake and that there had to be some problem with the computer or some other external issue ... only to realize way too late that I was indeed the origin of the mistake. Although it can be hard to admit fault sometimes, when it comes to your word vs that of the computer, the computer will win 99.99% of the time. So please save yourself the time and just start debugging. If you run into that 0.01%, feel free to send me a very angry email.

Or take a nap

Ok, maybe not literally, but it is good to recognize when you are tunneling counterproductively and need to temporarily step away. This is a little hard for me personally because I feel like my natural instinct is to completely stick with a problem until it is solved. However, a lot of times, the problem is solved only because we leave and are able to come back with a new and refreshed perspective on how to approach it.

Phone a friend

Speaking of new perspectives, what better than that of a friend? Even if they aren't totally familar with your project or don't have a programming background in general, it can still be really helpful to verbalize what your issue is to another person. In doing so, you might realize that things that made sense in your head actually don't make so much sense now that you've said them out loud.
